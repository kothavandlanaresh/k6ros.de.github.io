Derivation of \( \mathbf{A} \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{B} \) from Poisson's Equation

1. Background: Poisson's Equation

Poisson's Equation is given by:

\[ \nabla^2 \phi = -\frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0} \]

Where \( \nabla^2 \) is the Laplacian operator, \( \phi \) is the potential function, \( \rho \) is the source term (charge density), and \( \epsilon_0 \) is the permittivity of free space.

2. Minimization of Potential Energy

In the context of finite element analysis (FEM), we approach the problem by minimizing the total potential energy of the system.

The potential energy functional \( \Pi \) is given by:

\[ \Pi(\phi) = \frac{1}{2} \int_\Omega \nabla \phi \cdot \nabla \phi \, d\Omega - \int_\Omega \phi \cdot \frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0} \, d\Omega \]

To find the equilibrium solution, we seek to minimize \( \Pi(\phi) \) by solving:

\[ \frac{\delta \Pi(\phi)}{\delta \phi} = 0 \]

3. Derivation of the Discrete System \( \mathbf{A} \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{B} \)

In FEM, the domain \(\Omega\) is discretized into smaller elements. The continuous problem is approximated by:

Approximation: The potential \( \phi \) is approximated by a finite set of basis functions:

\[ \phi \approx \sum_{i=1}^n \phi_i \, N_i(\mathbf{x}) \]

Weak Formulation: We convert the PDE to its weak form by multiplying with a test function \( v \) and integrating:

\[ \int_\Omega \nabla \phi \cdot \nabla v \, d\Omega = \int_\Omega \frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0} \cdot v \, d\Omega \]

Matrix Form: The weak form is converted into matrix form:

\[ \mathbf{K} \mathbf{u} = \mathbf{F} \]

Where \( \mathbf{K} \) is the stiffness matrix and \( \mathbf{F} \) is the force vector.

4. Strong Form vs Weak Form

Strong Form: The original Poisson's equation:

\[ \nabla^2 \phi = -\frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0} \]

Weak Form: The weak form:

\[ \int_\Omega \nabla \phi \cdot \nabla v \, d\Omega = \int_\Omega \frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0} \cdot v \, d\Omega \]

The weak form accommodates irregular geometries and boundary conditions more flexibly.
