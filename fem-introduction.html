Why Minimizing Potential Energy Gives Us the AX=B Form

In many physical systems, especially in structural analysis and mechanics, we seek to minimize potential energy to find stable equilibrium configurations. This minimization can often be expressed as a system of equations of the form \( \mathbf{A}\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b} \).

The Principle of Minimum Potential Energy

The potential energy \( \Pi \) of a system is typically the sum of the strain energy and the potential of external forces. If we denote the displacement vector as \( \mathbf{u} \), the potential energy can be written as:

\[ \Pi(\mathbf{u}) = \frac{1}{2} \mathbf{u}^T \mathbf{K} \mathbf{u} - \mathbf{u}^T \mathbf{f} \]

Where:

  • \( \mathbf{K} \) is the stiffness matrix (related to the material properties and geometry).
  • \( \mathbf{f} \) is the vector of external forces applied to the system.

Deriving the Equilibrium Condition

To find the equilibrium, we minimize the potential energy with respect to the displacement vector \( \mathbf{u} \). This requires taking the derivative of \( \Pi(\mathbf{u}) \) with respect to \( \mathbf{u} \) and setting it to zero:

\[ \frac{d\Pi}{d\mathbf{u}} = \mathbf{K} \mathbf{u} - \mathbf{f} = 0 \]

Which simplifies to the linear system:

\[ \mathbf{K} \mathbf{u} = \mathbf{f} \]

This is in the form \( \mathbf{A} \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b} \), where:

  • \( \mathbf{A} \) corresponds to the stiffness matrix \( \mathbf{K} \).
  • \( \mathbf{x} \) corresponds to the displacement vector \( \mathbf{u} \).
  • \( \mathbf{b} \) corresponds to the external force vector \( \mathbf{f} \).

Conclusion

Thus, minimizing the potential energy of a system leads to a linear system of equations of the form \( \mathbf{A} \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b} \), which can be solved to find the displacements or other unknowns.
