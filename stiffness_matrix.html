Stiffness Matrix

Introduction

The stiffness matrix is a fundamental concept in FEM. It represents the relationship between nodal displacements and forces in a finite element system.

Derivation of the Stiffness Matrix

For a 1D bar element, the stiffness matrix can be derived using the principle of virtual work. The general form of the stiffness matrix for an element is:

        K = E * A / L * [1 -1
                         -1  1]

Where E is the Young's modulus, A is the cross-sectional area, and L is the length of the element.

Example: 2D Triangular Element

For a 2D triangular element, the stiffness matrix is more complex and involves the area of the triangle and the derivatives of the shape functions. It is generally represented as:

        K = (t / 4A) * [a1^2 + a2^2  a1*a2
                        a1*a2  a2^2 + a3^2]

Where t is the thickness, A is the area of the triangle, and a1, a2, a3 are the lengths of the sides.

Summary

The stiffness matrix is critical for solving FEM problems as it determines how forces are distributed across the elements and is used to assemble the global stiffness matrix.
